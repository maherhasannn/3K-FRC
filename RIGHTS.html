What are my rights in California?

In California, homeowners facing foreclosure have several important rights. They must receive a Notice of Default before foreclosure proceedings begin and a Notice of Trustee Sale before their property is auctioned. Homeowners have the right to cure the default by paying overdue amounts, request a loan modification to adjust loan terms, and participate in foreclosure mediation in some counties. While California does not typically offer a post-sale redemption period, homeowners can challenge improper foreclosures in court and seek legal advice. Additionally, they may be eligible for relocation assistance in certain cases. Understanding and utilizing these rights can help homeowners manage the foreclosure process and explore alternatives to losing their property.

Understanding Your Rights During the Foreclosure Process in California

Why Foreclosure Happens:

Foreclosure occurs when a homeowner is unable to keep up with mortgage payments. In California, the most common foreclosure process is non-judicial, meaning the lender can foreclose without going to court.

Your Rights During Foreclosure:

Right to Notice: Before foreclosure, lenders must send a Notice of Default (NOD), giving you 90 days to bring the mortgage current before they can proceed with the foreclosure.

Right to Reinstate: In California, homeowners can reinstate their loan by paying off the overdue amount (plus fees) up to five business days before the foreclosure sale.

Right to Redemption: In some cases, after the foreclosure sale, you may have a limited period to buy back your property by paying the full amount of the loan, including fees.

Right to Avoid Deficiency Judgments: California law prevents lenders from seeking a deficiency judgment for most residential properties, meaning they can't pursue you for the difference between what you owe and the foreclosure sale price.

Right to Relocation Assistance: Under the federal Protecting Tenants at Foreclosure Act and some state laws, tenants in foreclosed properties may be entitled to relocation assistance or more time to move out.
